      <h1>WIPP Update</h1>
        <h6 align="left"><strong>July 29, 2016<br /></strong></h6>
        <p align="center"><strong><br />
        </strong><strong>WIPP Mine Rescue Team Wins Field Competition at National  Championship</strong></p>
<table width="380" border="0">
  <tr>
    <td><img src="Images/Blue Team.jpg" width="380" height="285" alt="Blue Team" /></td>
  </tr>
  <tr>
    <td><em>The WIPP Blue Mine Rescue Team  poses with their national championship trophy in the field competition.  Pictured are (front row, left to right) Ty Zimmerly, Kirk Nance, Heath Fowler,  Richard (Stik) West and Phil Breidenbach, NWP President and Project Manager.  (Back row) Kenny Padilla, DOE Carlsbad Field Office Mine Operations Manager, Ed  Kyser, Manny Marquez, Nico Dominguez, Chris Dominguez, Lance Turnbow, Gary  Kessler and David Stuhan, NWP Emergency Operations and Security Manager. </em></td>

<p align="center">&nbsp;</p>
<p align="center">The  Waste Isolation Pilot Plant (WIPP) Blue Mine Rescue Team captured top honors in  the field competition at the Metal/Non-Metal National Mine Rescue Championship,  topping 36 teams from 18 states, spanning from Alaska to Georgia, who were  competing this week in Reno, Nevada. Newmont Mining Corporation from Carlin,  Nevada, edged out WIPP for the overall national championship.</p>
<p align="center">&ldquo;I  couldn&rsquo;t be prouder of the professionals we call the WIPP Mine Rescue Team,&rdquo;  said Phil Breidenbach, President and Project Manager of Nuclear Waste  Partnership, the management and operating contractor whose employees  represented WIPP at the national competition. &ldquo;After watching the competition  and seeing our employees&rsquo; passion, it makes me proud to be associated with  them. Every person at WIPP can rest assured that we work with the best mine  rescue teams in the nation.&rdquo;</p>
<p align="left">The Blue Team was  led by Team Captain Heath Fowler. He recently took over team captain duties  from Gary Kessler, who was inducted into  the Metal/Non-Metal National Mine Rescue Hall of Fame two years ago. The Mine  Safety and Health Administration (MSHA) established the Hall of Fame to honor  individuals who through their accomplishments and commitment stand out as  exceptional in the field of mine rescue.</p>
<p align="left">The Blue Team also finished fourth in the team  technician competition, which assesses the ability of two technicians to ensure  the multi-gas and self-contained breathing apparatuses are in proper working  condition.</p>
<p align="left">WIPP&rsquo;s Red Mine Rescue Team, which also  participated in the national championship, placed sixth in the first aid  competition, which requires individuals to deal with medical emergency  techniques.</p>
<p align="left">The National Mine Rescue Championship is held  every other year. Teams are tested on knowledge of mine gases, ventilation,  first-aid, miner recovery, and firefighting. Points are deducted according to  the types and seriousness of infractions. In other words, teams are penalized  more for life-threatening mistakes than for minor procedural mistakes. </p>
<p>Federal law  requires every operating mine in the United States to have access to two mine  rescue teams. Mine rescuers are highly-trained specialists with life-saving  skills they hope they'll never need to use. In addition to rescue capabilities,  WIPP&rsquo;s Blue and Red mine rescue teams support (and are supported by) personnel  from area potash mines through Memorandums of Understanding. This ensures that  mines in the same region have access to capable, highly-trained teams in the  event of an emergency.</p>
<p>Mine rescue competitions are designed  to sharpen skills and test the knowledge of team members in a mine emergency.  The contests require team members to solve hypothetical mine emergency problems,  such as fires, explosions or cave-ins, while being timed and observed by judges  from the Mine Safety and Health Administration.</p>
<p>At WIPP and most other mines, mine  rescue team members consist of volunteers who report to work every day,  performing normal tasks. The teams are composed of hoisting supervisors,  mechanics, waste handlers, mining operators, and other occupations. Teams  practice and prepare for the day they are called to a real emergency. These  volunteers devote countless hours, both before and after work, honing their  skills.</p>
<p align="left">Mine rescue teams participate in several  competitions each year to sharpen their skills, share lessons learned and  continually raise the level of mine safety in the world. In real emergencies,  the lives of mine rescue team members and their coworkers depend on the  proficiency of each individual&rsquo;s skill and training.</p>
<p align="left"><strong><em>Blue Team: </em></strong><em>Heath Fowler, Ed Keyser, Ty  Zimmerly, Kirk Nance, Nico Dominguez, Lance Tunbow, Gary Kessler, Manny Marquez  and Richard (Stik) West (trainer).</em></p>
<p><strong><em>Red Team: </em></strong><em>Matt Ridgway, Mark Long, Fabian  Carrasco, Matt Carnathan, Justin Bailey, Tony Mihelic, Aaron Ballew, Kelly  Burke and Richard (Stik) West (trainer).</em></p>
